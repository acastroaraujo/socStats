Resources

These are my personal recommendations for resources to start getting interested in statistics. It’s somewhat incomplete—e.g., there are no dedicated textbooks to causal inference, which is what we’ll cover next semester.

Class Resources:

  • R for Data Science (Wickham, Çetinkaya-Rundel, and Grolemund 2023)

    I learned a lot using the first edition of this book. Feel free to skip some chapters on a first pass and come back to them if you think you might need them (e.g., strings, regular expressions, webscraping). Also, I suggest you start with chapters 29 and 30.

  • The tidyverse style guide

    It will help you write pretty code.

  • Data Visualization (Healy 2018)

    It will help you make good graphs.

Good books to play around with:

  • Data Analysis for Social Science: A Friendly and Practical Introduction (Llaudet and Imai 2022)

    Very introductory but useful.

  • Statistical Inference via Data Science (Ismay and Kim 2019)

    It’s good!

  • Quantitative Social Science: An Introduction in Tidyverse (Imai and Williams 2022)

    I read this one a while ago, before it was re-written in tidyverse dialect. The chapters on probability and uncertainty are a great self-contained introduction to probability and statistical inference.

  • Regression and Other Stories (Gelman, Hill, and Vehtari 2020)

    This one seems a little too advanced for a first pass, but not advanced enough for a second pass? I like it a lot though.

Advanced Resources:

  • Advanced R (Wickham 2019).

    This one is good for those of you that have a background in computer science and are looking for reasons to like R. It’s also good for those of you who finished (most of) R4DS and want to learn some computer science concepts.

  • Introduction to probability (Blitzstein and Hwang 2019)

    Covers more probability theory than what you’ll probably need, but it’s a fascinating topic and it’s very accessible.

  • Statistical Rethinking (McElreath 2020)

    It’s what the cool kids are into these days.

  • Modern Statistics for Modern Biology (Holmes and Huber 2018)

    Great book, very different. But the examples are a bit outside of what we usually encounter in social science.
